Success by Subtraction: How I Applied the Principle of Inversion to Fix a Broken Product

When growth stalls, the instinct is always the same: add a feature, add a workflow, add a campaign. But for most products, the fastest growth lever isn‚Äôt additive at all ‚Äî it‚Äôs Inversion.

Human beings are wired for addition.

When a product isn't growing, our instinct is to add. Add a new feature, add a referral program, add a pop-up. We equate "solving a problem" with "doing more things."

But in my experience, the most powerful growth lever is usually the opposite. It is Inversion.

Instead of asking, "How do I make this product successful?", I ask, "What is currently making it fail?"

This isn't just a clever turn of phrase. It is a fundamental mental model championed by the mathematician Carl Jacobi ("Invert, always invert") and popularized by Charlie Munger.

When I applied this to a stalling SaaS product, we didn't ship a single new feature for a quarter. Instead, we removed the barriers to value. The result wasn't just stability; it was a 12% lift in growth. Here is the logic‚Äîand the math‚Äîbehind why this works.

The Mathematics of the "Leaky Bucket"

The economic argument for Inversion rests on the asymmetry between Acquisition and Retention.

According to research by Bain & Company (and corroborated by Harvard Business Review), increasing customer retention rates by just 5% increases profits by 25% to 95%.

Why? Because the cost of acquiring a new customer (CAC) is expensive and front-loaded, while the cost of retaining a customer drops over time.

If you have a "leaky bucket" (high churn), pouring more water in (Growth Hacking/Acquisition) is a waste of capital. You are paying to acquire users who will inevitably leave because you haven't fixed the "Death Moments" in your product.

The Science of Via Negativa

Nassim Taleb describes this concept as Via Negativa: improvement by subtraction.

In medicine, stopping a smoker from smoking adds more years to their life than giving them the best vitamins in the world. In product, removing a 3-second delay in your login screen adds more value than a new dashboard widget.

Amazon proved this decades ago. Their internal data showed that every 100ms of latency cost them 1% in sales. They didn't grow by making the store "prettier"; they grew by removing the friction of time.

How I Implement Inversion Operationally

It is easy to say "remove friction." It is hard to do it when your roadmap is full of shiny new features. Here is the operational framework I use to force Inversion:

1\. The "Reverse Brainstorm" In our quarterly planning, I hold a session with a simple prompt: "It is 12 months from now, and our product has failed completely. We have zero users left. What did we do to cause this?"

The team‚Äôs answers are usually illuminating:

  • "We annoyed them with too many emails."

  • "The mobile app login kept timing out."

  • "We made the export feature hard to find to lock them in."

This list becomes our "Anti-Roadmap." Our immediate priority becomes preventing these scenarios.

2\. The Friction Audit (The "Death Moments") I look at the funnel data to find the "Death Moments"‚Äîthe specific screens where user behavior drops to zero.

In one case, we found a 40% drop-off at a "Invite Your Team" screen during onboarding.

  • Additive Thinking: "Let's add a video explaining why teams are great!"

  • Inversion Thinking: "Why is this hard? Oh, they have to go look up email addresses."

  • The Fix: We removed the step entirely and let them invite via a link later. Conversion skyrocketed.

3\. The "Kill List" Every quarter, we identify features that are "net negatives"‚Äîfeatures that generate support tickets but low usage. We kill them. This reduces technical debt and cognitive load for the user. As Steve Jobs noted, "Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things."

The Psychological Edge

There is a final reason Inversion works: Loss Aversion.

Psychologically, users hate frustration (loss of time/patience) more than they love delight (gain of features). A user will tolerate a lack of "dark mode," but they will rage-quit over a broken password reset link.

By focusing on Inversion, you are solving for the user's strongest emotional triggers.

If your growth has plateaued, stop adding. Start subtracting. Find the anchors dragging you down and cut the rope.
