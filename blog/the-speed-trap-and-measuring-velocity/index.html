The Speed Trap: Why I Stopped Measuring "Shipping" and Started Measuring Velocity

Most startups confuse "Speed" (shipping code) with "Velocity" (shipping value). In this deep dive, I break down the physics of product management and show why 80% of features are "digital waste." Learn how to use "Vector Checks" to align your team and stop running fast in the wrong direction.

In the startup world, "Move Fast and Break Things" became the religion of the last decade. Speed was the ultimate virtue. If you weren't shipping daily, you were dying.

But in 2025, I see companies moving faster than ever, yet they are standing still. They are burning out engineering teams, shipping record numbers of features, and seeing zero movement in their North Star metrics.

They are confusing Speed with Velocity.

As an engineering leader, learning the distinction between these two physics concepts was the single most important mental model shift of my career. One leads to burnout; the other leads to leverage.

The Physics of the Problem: Scalars vs. Vectors

To understand why teams fail despite working hard, we have to look at the math.

  • Speed is a Scalar Quantity. It is defined simply as distance divided by time ($Speed = Distance / Time$). It has magnitude, but no direction. Running on a treadmill at 10mph requires high speed, but your displacement is zero.

  • Velocity is a Vector Quantity. It is defined as displacement divided by time in a specific direction. It requires both magnitude AND direction.

If you have a team of 10 engineers working at maximum capacity (High Speed), but 5 are building for Enterprise clients and 5 are building for SMBs in a way that contradicts the Enterprise architecture, their vectors cancel each other out.

Net Velocity ‚âà 0.

You have paid for the speed (salaries, server costs, coffee), but you received zero velocity.

The Data: The Trillion-Dollar Waste

This isn't just a metaphor; it is a measurable financial hemorrhage.

According to a landmark report by Pendo (analyzing 1,000+ digital products), 80% of features in the average software product are rarely or never used.

  • 12% are used daily.

  • 8% are used weekly.

  • 80% are "digital waste."

Think about the implications of that data. If your team ships 10 features this quarter, statistically, 8 of them will generate zero value.

If you are managing based on "Speed" (e.g., "How many tickets did we close?"), you are incentivizing the creation of that waste. You are celebrating the production of code that no one wants. This is the "Feature Factory" trap.

The Cost of Misalignment

The cost isn't just wasted salary; it is complexity.

Every line of code you ship introduces:

  1. Maintenance overhead.

  2. Potential bugs.

  3. Cognitive load for the user.

When you prioritize speed over velocity, you accrue Technical Debt and Product Debt simultaneously. You build a fast car that is getting heavier with every mile, eventually making it impossible to steer.

How I Implement Velocity Checks Operationally

To fix this, I stopped asking "How fast are we moving?" and started asking "Where are we going?" Here is the operational framework:

1. The "Vector Check" in Sprint Planning

I implemented a rule: No ticket enters a sprint unless it is explicitly tagged to a Key Result (KR).

If an engineer picks up a task, they must be able to answer: "Which vector does this support?"

If the answer is "I don't know" or "It's just a cool idea," the ticket is killed. This ensures every ounce of force is applied in the same direction.

2. Measuring "Outcome over Output"

We stopped measuring success by Output (number of features shipped).

We started measuring success by Outcome (change in user behavior).

  • Bad KPI: "Shipped the new dashboard." (Speed).

  • Good KPI: "Reduced time-to-insight by 15%." (Velocity).

If we ship the dashboard and time-to-insight doesn't change, we failed‚Äîeven if we shipped it "fast."

3. The "Kill Rate" Metric

I track a metric called "Kill Rate"‚Äîthe percentage of features or code we remove from the product.

High-velocity teams don't just add; they subtract to maintain aerodynamic efficiency. If we aren't deleting old code, we are accumulating drag.

The Takeaway

Speed is satisfying. It feels like work. It makes the Slack channel noisy and the Jira burndown chart look pretty.

But Velocity is what moves the needle.

In a world of infinite distraction and limited resources, the most dangerous thing you can do is run fast in the wrong direction. Stop optimizing for the treadmill. Start optimizing for the map.
