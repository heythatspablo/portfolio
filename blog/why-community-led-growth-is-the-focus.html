Why "Community-Led Growth" is the focus

In an era where AI makes software easy to copy, features are no longer a defensible moat. I argue that the only thing you can't fork is a network of humans. Here is my playbook for shifting from "Product-Led Growth" to "Community-Led Growth."

For the last decade, the playbook for SaaS success was dangerously simple: Product-Led Growth (PLG).

The logic was elegant: Build a best-in-class product, remove friction with a free tier, and rely on the product value alone to drive the upgrade. In this era, the code itself was the moat. If your engineering was harder to replicate than your competitors, you won.

But in 2025, that moat hasn't just evaporated; it has been paved over.

Generative AI has collapsed the marginal cost of software creation to near zero. Today, if you launch a unique, game-changing feature, a competitor‚Äîor a 19-year-old in a dorm room with an LLM agent‚Äîcan clone it by next Tuesday.

Features are no longer differentiators; they are table stakes. Interfaces are commodities. Even complex backend logic is becoming a commodity.

So, what is left when the code is free? What is the one asset that cannot be forked on GitHub, copied by a bot, or pasted into a competitor's codebase?

The Network. The messy, complex, high-friction web of relationships between your users. The code serves the people, but the people serve each other.

This is why I am shifting my entire strategy to Community-Led Growth (CLG).

The Shift: From Audience to Community

Most founders confuse "Audience" with "Community," using the terms interchangeably. This is a fatal mistake.

  • Audience: You stand on a stage and speak. They listen. It is a One-to-Many relationship. The value flows in one direction: from you to them. (Metric: Reach)

  • Community: You step off the stage. They speak to each other. You facilitate. It is a Many-to-Many relationship. The value is a mesh network.

An audience is fragile because it is transactional; it is entirely dependent on your output. If you stop posting, stop emailing, or stop performing, they leave. The moment the spotlight turns off, the room clears.

A community is resilient; it has its own heartbeat. If you step away for a month, you should return to find them still talking, debating, and helping one another. An audience consumes value; a community creates it.

Why Community is the New Moat

In a world of infinite software supply, the only scarcity is trust and connection. Here is why CLG protects your business:

1\. Support Velocity (The "Stack Overflow" Effect)

When you have a thriving community, your users become your support tier. They work 24/7, they speak the user's language, and they do it for free.

At Unfamiliar.id, we created a Discord channel for developers. Within months, I noticed a shift. When a new user asked a technical question at 2 AM, a veteran user would debug it and provide a solution before my support team even logged in for the morning.

This achieves two things:

  • Drastically reduced support costs.

  • Increased "Time to Value." New users don't get stuck in the "ticket queue"; they get unblocked instantly by peers. This peer-to-peer validation is often trusted more than official documentation.

2\. Retention via Social Capital (The "Identity" Lock-in)

People come for the tool, but they stay for the network.

If I switch from Figma to a cheaper competitor, I don't just lose the tool. I lose the ecosystem of plugins, the shared templates, the tutorials created by other users, and the common language I have with other designers.

But more importantly, I lose my status. In a community, users build reputation‚Äîbadges, post counts, thank-you's, and recognition. This is Social Capital. You cannot export your reputation to a new tool. Leaving the community means declaring "social bankruptcy" and starting over as a nobody. That is the ultimate retention hook.

3\. The Feedback Loop (De-risking the Roadmap)

A community doesn't just complain; they co-create.

In the old model, product managers guessed what users wanted. In CLG, you simply ask. By opening up our roadmap to our "Power User Group," we stopped building features in a vacuum. We let them vote, debate, and beta test.

This creates the IKEA Effect: we value things more when we help build them. When your users feel ownership over the product's direction, they don't churn when something breaks. They stick around to help you fix it because it feels like their product too.

The Playbook: How to Build It

Building community is harder than buying ads. It cannot be automated. It requires high emotional labor. Here is my framework for building it right:

Phase 1: The "Campfire" (Curated Access)

The biggest mistake is launching a public Slack, posting the link on Twitter, and hoping for the best. That leads to a ghost town, or worse, a noisy room full of spam.

Start small. This is the Campfire phase. Invite your top 50 'high-signal' users‚Äîthose who provide the most constructive feedback, not necessarily the highest spenders‚Äîto a private space. Give them exclusive access to you and the roadmap. Make it feel like a secret society.

Goal:* Density. You want a small room where everyone talks, not a stadium where everyone is silent. You are building the "DNA" of the culture here.

Phase 2: The "Rituals" (Habit Formation)

Communities don't happen by accident; they happen through rhythm. You need Rituals‚Äîpredictable events that bring people back.

  • Weekly "Show and Tell": A thread every Friday where users show off what they built with your tool. This inspires others and gives the creators a dopamine hit of validation.

  • Monthly "Town Hall": A live voice call with the founders. No scripts, just Q&A. This builds radical transparency.

  • Annual "Awards": Recognize your top contributors publicly. Send them physical swag. Make them feel seen.

Rituals turn a "group chat" into a "tribe."

Phase 3: The "Platform" (Scale and Decentralization)

Only once the culture is set‚Äîand the campfire is roaring‚Äîdo you open the floodgates. This is the Platform phase.

At this stage, you cannot moderate everything yourself. You must introduce "Ambassador Programs." You identify the leaders from Phase 1 and 2 and give them power. You reward them with status (badges, exclusive channels, discounts) in exchange for moderating and welcoming new members.

You are no longer the host of the party; you are the owner of the venue. The community runs itself.

The Takeaway

In the AI era, software is cheap. Connection is the currency.

If you want to build a business that lasts through the coming wave of commoditization, stop optimizing for "users" and start optimizing for "members." Build a club that people are proud to belong to.

If you build a great product, you might survive 2025. If you build a great community, you will thrive in it.
